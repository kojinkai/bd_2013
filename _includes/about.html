About Me

I specialise in Front End Development combined with Visual Design and I have enjoyed my experiences of working within agile teams at the intersection of design & development. I'm currently looking for a new permanent role but will consider a minimum 3 month contractor position.

Web standards and accessibility form the basis of my HTML code and that generally means clean, semantically rich, well structured markup. For the sake of maintainability and performance, I also aim to keep functionally discrete parts of a website or app separated. That is to say HTML for describing the document structure, CSS for presentational styling and JavaScript for event handling, AJAX calls and other behavioural enhancements. This is a solid foundation and important stepping stone in the path towards maintainable and accessible Front End code.

I'm also obsessed with performance, not only in terms of site speed but also in terms of the design performing on the multitude of web enabled devices in circulation today. This is undoubtably one of the biggest shifts in recent times for Front End developers and UI designers. It's a shift that continues to throw up interesting problems and solutions, with new mobile design patterns now emerging and Front End performance now common in discussions within this working space.

With that in mind, I'm constantly mindful of all the new things there are to learn about this career and never rest on my laurels, instead choosing to constantly operate at the sharp edge of my expertise, absorbing all the best-practices and wisdom handed down from the industry thought-leaders as I continue to step forward and become a better pro every day.

About this Site

This site is powered by a site generation tool called Jekyll which is a framework authored and maintained by the team at Github. It's the recommended framework for creating custom Github pages and that is indeed how I discovered Jekyll - by creating the Github page for my current organisation, Dachis Group UK.

I found it really easy to get to grips with and so it became a natural fit for creating a static site such as this. It's easily installed as a RubyGem and can get you moving quickly and effortlessly. The documentation is excellent and has to-date been a pleasure to work with. Having said that, compiling and compressing the CSS and JavaScript quickly became an issue, but more on that later.

With this site I have aimed to reduce the amount of clutter in the HTML and by farming a lot of the classes out to SASS Mixins I aimed to keep the HTML as lean and meaningful as possible. This was inspired by the neat SASS grid framework I adopted for this project.

Along with this framwork I opted to use Bourbon, a SASS mixin framework for a leg-up with vendor-prefixed properties and highly readable SASS stylesheets. I would recommend this approach highly, again, both frameworks are easily installed as RubyGems and can get you set up in a matter of minutes. Having previously authored custom CSS frameworks for a few projects, I can't emphasise enough how much I appreciated the elegance and simplicity of this approach. It really allows you to focus on the more difficult custom aspects of the site.

Speaking of difficult aspects, I quickly found that asset compilation was becoming an issue that was looming large from the get-go so I opted to tackle it right away.

My experience with working on several Ruby on in exisRails projects has fostered a deep appreciation for the asset pipeline and although there is a third-party asset pipeline for Jekyll in existence, I opted instead to better my understanding of Grunt.js, the excellent and popular JavaScript task runner.

Grunt is currently configured to run jshint on the JavaScript - which in my opinion is far better than running it from Sublime Text (as I had done previously) - and compile the SASS and concatenate and minify the JavaScript. I used it to dive into unit tests with qUnit on the jQuery plugins I developed for this site. As an aside, unit testing is another area I'm keen to explore, with Mocha, Chai and Sinon seemingly proving popular testing libraries with JavaScript developers who practice BDD.

Grunt is a tool i'm really pleased to have begun to integrate as its a real safety net ensuring good code quality. It takes care of all the asset compression and helps you achieve the optimisation we so often talk about as a central tenet of good Front End Development. It's also unobtrusive and I have used it successfully on a number of projects now.

About Developing for the Mobile Landscape

Taking into consideration the huge variety of web-enabled devices has evolved from being a bolt-on component or even out-of-scope, to a central project requirement and UX consideration. This has been my experience as new and existing clients have come to us with new campaign ideas as well as enhancements to existing platforms. It's clear that the now feted Responsive Design paradigm is a preferable option in most cases over maintaining a separate mobile site on a sub-domain. Within Dachis Group, where I work, this responsibility has fallen largely upon my shoulders and so most of the business logic of catering for the many devices in existence today has fallen withing the domain of UI Design and Front End Development.

This has presented me with an interesting problem. How can you take the same content, complete with one minified scripts package to cut down on HTTP overhead, and serve that up to your mobile users with the knowledge that most of the functionality they will be getting served either won't enhance their experience or not be executed at all?

I'm certainly not the first person to wrangle with this catch 22-type situation and so for this site I took a bit of advice from the developers who built the now infamous Boston Globe website. After reading the blog post they authored after the fact, I began thinking about how I could enhance the experience for desktop users with higher bandwidth and faster connections and cut down the network overhead to a bare minimum for mobile devices. I already had Modernizr.js being served up as part of my development boilerplate so I knew that yepnope.js was being bundled along with it. So I set about writing a test that would determine wheter a mobile device was requesting the page, and if it was, asynchronously serve up an enhancements package. This package was in effect mainly jQuery plugins that only made sense with larger screens or hooked into scroll events that were either buggy or unneccesary on touch-enabled devices.

This was achieved by doing a quick Regex match on the User Agent string. This approach is not without it's pitfalls, and feature detection is still a better option for desktop browsers. But I have read on a couple of occasions that this is still the best way to tailor an experience for mobile, rather than doing complex feature detection in a runaway mobile landscape.

Along with this, I loaded the navigation menu within the yepnope JavaScript load callback. The menu became an unneccesary UI component for mobile and tablet so I chose to do away with it entirely on tablets and mobile devices. I also wanted to preserve the full page splash effect for tablets that I thought looked really nice.

Designing for Scalability

So with all of the above in mind, I wanted to ensure that my graphics were crisp and precise even on hi-resolution displays. I had already been a big advocate of using SVG as they are lightweight and fairly easy to polyfill for older browsers. This felt perfect for the Box Deluxe logo I had worked on and ensured that it reproduced beautifully across all the displays I tested it on.

However, the most interesting thing I used was a custom font set for the UI components and social media icons. I used theIcomoon app to generate a lightweight custom font from the icons I had designed in Illustrator. This allowed me to trim significant HTTP requests and also include only the icons I actually needed in a custom set. I can also keep my icon sets stored within the app ready for download or modification. It really is an excellent app, just another part of the toolkit that continues to astound me, it's really fantastic, and free!

If you want to get a better idea of how this site is assembled then check out the Github repository, all the code is up there.

A Note on Visual Design

So far I have neglected to talk about the visual design which is probably a symptom of the fact that I adopted so many new ideas in the Front End Development aspect of the build. That said, I should touch on it briefly.

Throughout this process, I felt it was important not to design in a vacuum - that is to say that I should consider performance and accessibility as much a design concern as a Front End concern. I think this is where much of the benfit of having design oriented Front End Developers is really seen.

The main requirement of this site was to showcase my Front End Capabilites as well as my Design Skills and so crisp, bold colours and sharp vector lines not only added impact visually, but also offered enhanced performance in terms of leaning heavily on CSS3 rendering which in turn meant fewer HTTP requests in this case.

The design was a nod towards the current tendency towards the so-called flat UI designs. I wanted elegance and simplicity but I also wanted relevance. Flat UI design may well be a reaction to our skeumorphic roots but it offers convenient performance gains I was looking for also.

But this is my site, my personality and the logo design was my opportunity to let some of that personality come through. I collect vintage guitars and amps and love the music of the 50's and 60's so I wanted to weave some of that distinctive era into my design. The main logo was designed using a typeface called Cocktail Shaker and was a perfect fit. With a few tweaks to the vector lines, I had a new logo that really felt as though it summed up my personality. I have had the pleasure of designing a couple of logos during my time at Dachis Group and several prior to that so I called upon that experience to make something simple but aligning very comfortably with my style.
